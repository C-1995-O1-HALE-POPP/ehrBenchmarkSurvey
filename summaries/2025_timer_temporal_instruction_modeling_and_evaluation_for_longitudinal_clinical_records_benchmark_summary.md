## 1. MedAlign

MedAlign is a clinician-generated benchmark for instruction-following over longitudinal electronic health records, originally introduced by Fleming et al. (2024). The TIMER paper reuses MedAlign as an evaluation benchmark to assess temporal reasoning capabilities. MedAlign contains 983 clinician-authored instruction-response pairs across 276 patient EHRs from an academic medical center, covering a median temporal duration of 3,895.1 days (~10.7 years). The TIMER paper notes that MedAlign exhibits pronounced recency bias — 55.3% of instructions reference only the final 25% of patient timelines. TIMER-tuned models achieve consistent improvements on MedAlign (e.g., Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct correctness improves from 30.69% to 34.32%).

- **Language:** English (inferred — Stanford Medicine, USA source system)
- **Clinical Stage:** Longitudinal hospital course (multi-visit, spanning up to ~10.7 years)
- **Source Clinical Document Type:** Structured EHR serialized into XML (OMOP CDM), covering clinical notes, labs, medications, procedures, and diagnoses
- **Clinical Specialty:** Multi-specialty (Internal Medicine, Neurology, Radiology, Cardiology, Oncology, Surgery, Primary Care — 15 clinicians across 7 specialties)
- **Application Method:** Public benchmark (available on Stanford Redivis: https://stanford.redivis.com/datasets/48nr-frxd97exb); requires credentialed access for the underlying EHR data

## 2. TIMER-Eval

TIMER-Eval is a temporal reasoning evaluation benchmark introduced by the TIMER paper to systematically assess LLM capabilities for reasoning across longitudinal clinical records. Unlike existing benchmarks that exhibit recency bias, TIMER-Eval uses controlled temporal distribution sampling to generate instruction-response pairs grounded at specific points across patient timelines. The benchmark is model-generated (Gemini-1.5-Pro) using a specialized prompt template (`temporal_evaluation.txt`) that emphasizes temporal progression of clinical events. Each instruction requires evidence integration from at least two distinct timestamps. The benchmark was validated by three clinicians: clinical relevance 95/100, temporal reasoning complexity 80/100, factual accuracy 98/100 (Table 4). Three distribution patterns are defined: Recency-Focused (final 25% of timeline), Edge-Focused (timeline extremes), and Uniformly-Distributed (balanced across timeline).

- **Language:** English (inferred — generated from US-based EHR data)
- **Clinical Stage:** Longitudinal hospital course (multi-visit, median 1,294.9 days temporal coverage per the paper)
- **Source Clinical Document Type:** Structured EHR serialized into XML (OMOP CDM), generated from STARR (Stanford Medicine Research Data Repository)
- **Clinical Specialty:** Multi-specialty (general clinical temporal reasoning across various conditions)
- **Application Method:** Benchmark introduced by the paper. Code and generation prompts publicly available at https://github.com/som-shahlab/TIMER. Underlying clinical data requires institutional STARR access. A minimal synthetic dataset representative of the data structure will be released publicly alongside the code.

### 2.1 Task: Temporal Reasoning QA

This task is to answer clinical questions that require synthesizing evidence across multiple timepoints in a patient's longitudinal electronic health record. Questions are designed to require genuine temporal reasoning (not simple retrieval) — for example, comparing treatment response over time, tracking lab trends across visits, or assessing disease progression from one time period to another. Each question is grounded in specific timestamps within the patient record.

Longitudinal EHR serialized into XML format (OMOP CDM) from STARR, ordered by ascending clinical event timestamps. The patient timeline is segmented into 16,000-token context windows suitable for LLM processing. Each instruction-response pair is linked to specific temporal evidence via timestamps.
### Output
Free-text clinical response that demonstrates temporal reasoning — referencing specific dates, identifying trends over time, and correctly limiting analysis to the specified time periods. Responses must include specific timestamps (dates in MM/DD/YYYY format) to justify conclusions.
