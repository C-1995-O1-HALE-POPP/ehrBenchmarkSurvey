A BSTRACT
Biomedical language understanding benchmarks are the driving forces for artificial intelligence applications with large language model (LLM) back-ends. However, most current benchmarks: (a) are limited to English which makes it challenging to replicate many of the successes in English for other languages, or (b)
focus on knowledge probing of LLMs and neglect to evaluate how LLMs apply
these knowledge to perform on a wide range of bio-medical tasks, or (c) have
become a publicly available corpus and are leaked to LLMs during pre-training.
To facilitate the research in medical LLMs, we re-build the Chinese Biomedical Language Understanding Evaluation (CBLUE) benchmark into a large scale
prompt-tuning benchmark, PromptCBLUE. Our benchmark is a suitable test-bed
and an online platform for evaluating Chinese LLMs’ multi-task capabilities on a
wide range bio-medical tasks including medical entity recognition, medical text
classification, medical natural language inference, medical dialogue understanding and medical content/dialogue generation. To establish evaluation on these
tasks, we have experimented and report the results with the current 9 Chinese
LLMs fine-tuned with differtent fine-tuning techniques.1 Our benchmark is released at https://tianchi.aliyun.com/competition/entrance/
532084/introduction.

open-domain LLMs, there are also many works trying to develop LLMs that are specialized in the

Evaluation datasets and benchmarks is one of the key recipe for developing high quality LLMs in
the medical domain. In the pre-LLM era, there are many bio-medical benchmark datasets, including
PubMedQA (Jin et al., 2019), BioRead (Pappas et al., 2018), BLURB (Gu et al., 2020), MedQAUSMLE (Jin et al., 2020), Text2DT (Zhu et al., 2023) and the Chinese Biomedical Language Understanding Evaluation (CBLUE) benchmark. Recently, a series of medical LLM bechmarks are

analyzing the existing benchmark, we find that the existing benchmarks: (a) are limited to English
which makes it challenging to replicate many of the successes in English for other languages, or
(b) focus on knowledge probing of LLMs and neglect to evaluate how LLMs apply these knowledge to perform on a wide range of bio-medical natural language processing (BioNLP) tasks, or
(c) have become a publicly available corpus and are leaked to LLMs during pre-training, such as
MedQA-USMLE.
In order to address the above issues and facilitate medical LLM studies in Chinese, we re-build the
CBLUE benchmark into PromptCBLUE, a large-scale multi-task prompt tuning benchmark dataset.
PromptCBLUE is a suitable test-bed for Chinese medical LLMs for the following reasons: (a) we
evaluate Chinese LLMs’ multi-task capabilities on a wide range bio-medical tasks including medical entity recognition, medical text classification, medical natural language inference, medical dialogue understanding and medical content/dialogue generation. (b) PromptCBLUE’s test ground
truths are kept from the public corpus, which can effectively evaluate LLMs’ generalization and
instruction-following capabilities. (c) We host the dataset on an online platform for test evaluations
and leaderboard presentation.
To estable baseline evaluation on these tasks, we have experimented and reported the results of the
currently most powerful commercial and open-sourced models with different learning or fine-tuning
techniques. The following take-aways can be made: (a) despite its powerfulness, the OpenAI’s
ChatGPT and GPT-4 still fall behind fine-tuned open-sourced LLMs with smaller scales, showing that fine-tuning is still necessary toward applications.12 (b) chain-of-thought prompting and
output format design can help LLMs perform better on medical information extraction tasks of
PromptCBLUE. (c) Currently, we can not see how further-pretraining in the medical domain significantly improve the performance of LLMs, which is different with the observations in the BERT era
(Zhu et al., 2021a; Guo et al., 2021). We also conduct case studies to analyze when LLMs fail.
The PromptCBLUE benchmark is an on-going work, and we intend to expand the tasks and build
a more comprehensive GLUE like open platform for the Chinese BioNLP community. Overall, the
main contributions of this study are as follows:

• We propose the first prompted based Chinese biomedical language understanding benchmark, PromptCBLUE, on the basis of CBLUE benchmark. The proposed benchmark
serves as a platform for the development of Chinese LLMs for the medical domain. We
encourages new dataset contributions.

• We report a systematic evaluation of a series of Chinese LLMs and learning/fine-tuning
techniques, in order to understand the challenges derived by PromptCBLUE. We release
the source code of the baselines as a toolkit for future research purposes.

R ELATED WORK

2.1

B ENCHMARKS FOR LLM S

Benchmarking is essential for the advancement of natural language processing (NLP), especially in
the era (or post-era) of pre-trained language models (PLMs). Notably, pre-PLM era has witnessed
the development of many NLP models for a specific NLP task like reading comprehension (Rajpurkar et al., 2016), natural language inference (Bowman et al., 2015; Sadat & Caragea, 2022; Zhu,
2021a; Zhu et al., 2021b), named entity recognition (NER) Tjong Kim Sang & De Meulder (2003);
Weischedel et al. (2013), relation extraction (Zhu, 2021b), and other classic benchmarking tasks
(Barnes et al., 2022; Walker et al., 2006; Zuo et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2023a; Gao et al., 2023a).
With the rise of LMs like BERT (Devlin et al., 2019), RoBERTa (Liu et al., 2019), ALBERT (Lan
et al., 2020) and Electra (Zeng et al., 2021; Guo et al., 2021), the benchmarks become multi-task.
The prominent examples are: (a) the GLUE benchmark (Wang et al., 2018), which consists of multiple types of tasks in the format of single-sentence classification and sentence-pair classification.
(b) SuperGLUE (Wang et al., 2019), which consists more challenging tasks and more types of tasks
compared to the GLUE benckmark.
With the development of larger pretrained models, many benchmarks have been conquered. Thus,
various bench-marking datasets have been proposed to evaluate the capabilities of pretrained LMs,
including NaturalQuestion (Kwiatkowski et al., 2019), MultiSpanQA (Li et al., 2022), commonsense
reasoning (Clark et al., 2018; Sakaguchi et al., 2021), mathematical reasoning (Cobbe et al., 2021)
and code generation (Zheng et al., 2023b).
With the widely spread of large language models (LLMs) like GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020), PaLM
(Chowdhery et al., 2022), ChatGPT13 , GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023), BARD 14 , CLAUDE 15 and ChatGLM16 , LLMs perform quite strong in some tasks such as summarization, translation, question
answering, code generation (Qin et al., 2023). Thus, it is in-appropriate to continue to use these established benchmarks for evaluations of LLMs. In order to comprehensively assess the capabilities
of LLMs, massive multi-task benchmarks are proposed, including MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020),
BBH (Srivastava et al., 2022). MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020) includes real-world exam questions
of different tasks from multiple domains. It is widely used in evaluating LLMs. However, All these
benchmarks are primarily focused on the English language.
Recently, Many evaluation benchmarks are proposed to evaluate the LLMs on Chinese, the language
with the highest number of speakers. AGIEval (Zhong et al., 2023) uses Chinese standardized exams
like college entrance exams, math competitions, and lawyer qualification tests to build a test set for
Chinese LLMs. In comparison, C-Eval (Huang et al., 2023) includes question at four different
education levels (elementary school, middle school, high school and college). CMMLU (Li et al.,
2023a) consists of many Chinese-specific question such as Chinese food culture, Chinese driving
rule. Our work complements the literature by providing a large scale prompt learning benchmark
for the Chinese biomedical NLP field.

2.3

B ENCHMARKING C HINESE MEDICAL TEXT PROCESSING

With the advances of language models, the community of Chinese bio-medical NLP has seen rapid
development. After the rise of BERT, a series of pre-trained models in the medical domain are pretrained or further pre-trained. For example, Guo et al. (2021) propose a novel pretraining technique
and obtain SOTA performances on Chinese spelling error correction tasks. Other representative Chinese medical pre-trained models includes Cai et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2021). With the advances
of Chinese medical PLMs, a large-scale, multi-task benchmark, CBLUE (Zhang et al., 2022), is
developed to provide a unified testbed for those PLMs. CBLUE consists of a wide range of biomedical language understanding tasks which are carefully annotated by human experts. However, the
CBLUE datasets does not come with the instruction tuning format, making it inconvenient to evaluate this benchmark on LLMs. Our PromptCBLUE benchmark builds upon CBLUE and transforms
all the CBLUE tasks to instruction-tuning formats via a large collection of prompt templates that are
validated by human experts.

P ROMPT CBLUE B ENCHMARK

In this section, we first explain our motivations in proposing our PromptCBLUE benchmark, and
then we will elaborate on the compositions of this benchmark.
3.1

M OTIVATION

Probing the medical knowledge of LLMs Knowledge, the information encompassing facts,
events, and skills, has long served as an central indicator for intelligence level of artificial intelligence (AI) (Yu et al., 2023). A series of works have been devoted in investigating whether LLMs
contains medical knowledge via large scale pretraining (Singhal et al., 2023a; Gao et al., 2023b;
Singhal et al., 2023b). However, these works have focused on English medical benchmark tasks,
while the evaluations on Chinese are neglected.
Capabilities of multi-task medical text understanding Nowadays, the medical application requires a system to be good at multiple tasks at once (Karabacak & Margetis, 2023). For example,
a system for medical query answering may requires the system to recognize the key entities in the
query, or is able to retrieve semantically related known queries or related medical documents that
could help to answer the queries, and summarize the medical knowledges, facts or observations to
respond to the users. In the the era of LLMs, whether a single model can perform various tasks are
widely investigated (Wei et al., 2021; Mishra et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022b; Chung et al., 2022;
Ouyang et al., 2022). The multi-task or cross-task generalization abilities will re-shape the research
and development processes in the industry.18 PromptCBLUE contains a wide range of Chinese biomedical tasks, thus providing a ideal testbed for developing and testing the capabilties of Chinese
LLMs for medical applications.
PromptSource for Chinese medical NLP community A large scale prompt set is of central
importance for the LLMs’ development. For example, PromptSource (Bach et al., 2022), Natural
Instructions (Mishra et al., 2022), and many other prompt tuning datasets play an important role in
facilitating the instruction tuning of LLMs. In the Chinese bio-medical domain, the instruction tuning datasets are relatively lacking. Many instruction datasets are developed via self-instruct (Wang
et al., 2022a), there are is still no large scale instruction datasets for Chinese medical text understanding. Thus, in PromptCBLUE, we construct a large prompt tuning dataset for Chinese medical
LLMs, which can facilitate and speedup the development and evaluation of Chinese medical LLMs.

3.2

OVERVIEW OF P ROMPT CBLUE

Built upon the CBLUE benchmark (Zhang et al., 2022), we created an extensive multi-task test suite
in the medical domain for the LLMs that supports Chinese. The tasks in PromptCBLUE can be
divided into the following groups:
• Medical information extraction, including: (a) CMeEE-V2 (Zan et al., 2020), a medical
named entity recognition task. (b) CMeIE (Guan et al., 2020), a medical triple extraction
task. (c) CHIP-CDEE, which asks models to extract clinical finding events; (d) CHIP-CDN,
which asks one to map the diagnosis descriptions to standard ICD-1019 disease terms. (e)
IMCS-V2-NER for extracting medical entities in the dialogues from the medical dialogue
datasets IMCS-V2 (Chen et al., 2022).
• Medical text classification, which includes: (a) CHIP-CTC (Zong et al., 2021), the classification task for Chinese eligibility criteria in clinical trials. (b) KUAKE-QIC, which
classifies online medical queries. (c) IMCS-V2-DAC for medical intent classification of
medical dialogues.
• Medical natural language inference tasks, including CHIP-STS (Luo et al., 2021),
KUAKE-QQR, KUAKE-IR and KUAKE-QTR tasks, which asks a model to determine
the semantic relations between a pair of medical queries or a query-document pair.
• Symptom status understanding for medical dialogues. Medical dialogues, like medical consultations, are centered around paitents’ symptoms. However, not every symptom
mentioned in the dialogues is related to patients or reflect patients’ current medical status.
Thus, in order to gain deep understanding of the doctor-patient dialogues, a model must be
able to extract the symptoms and their statuses. This cohort includes: (a) IMCS-V2-SR,
and (b) CHIP-MDCFNPC for extracting clinical findings and their status. This cohort of
tasks is based on the medical dialogue datasets IMCS-V2 (Chen et al., 2022) and CHIPMDCFNPC (Xiong et al., 2021).
• Medical content generation, which includes two generation tasks based on medical dialogues between patients and doctors: (a) IMCS-V2-MRG (Chen et al., 2022), which asks
LLMs to summarize the medical dialogues. (b) MedDG (Liu et al., 2020), which asks
LLMs to act like a doctor and respond to patient’s queries in a dialogue.
Prompt collection We employ both manual efforts and ChatGPT to collect prompt templates
for PromptCBLUE. The prompt templates mainly contains task instructions, that is, what we want
the LLMs provide given the text input. Firstly, each of the three annotators who are graduates
student majoring in computer science and are studying LLMs will write around 2 seed prompt
templates manually. We ask the annotators to write the prompts as diversified as possible. The
prompt templates are then reviewed by a review panel consisting of two medical experts and a
senior NLP researcher to ensure their validity. If a prompt is not proper (for example, not expressing
the task cleary), we will ask the annotators to modify it until it was accepted by the domain experts.
Then, we will ask the ChatGPT to rephrase each of the six seed prompts templates for 10 times
without changing the meaning or changing the placeholders in the templates. Then the generated
templates will be reviewed by the same panel of experts, and only the templates passing the reviews
will be added to the template pool. After the prompt collection process, they are totally 20

Response format Note that LLMs can only generate token sequences to represent their answers
to the queries, we have to transform the structured outputs of the original CBLUE tasks to natural
language sequences. In Appendix A.1, we present the target output formats for each task under
PromptCBLUE.
Sample format We use the prompt template pool to reformulate all the tasks in the CBLUE
benchmark to instruction-following generation tasks with the following data structure:
{
"input": str,
"target": str,
"answer_choices": Union[list, Nonetype],
"sample_id": str,
"task_type": str,
"task_dataset": str,
}
Here, input is the prompt sequence, target is the response we want the model to output, or at least
the model should generate responses that contains the answers to the prompt. The other four keys
are auxiliary, and they will not be used by the LLMs. sample id is the sample index. answer choices
is the label options allowed by the prompt. The value for this key will be None if the task does not
have a predefined label set. task dataset specifies the task name in the original CBLUE benchmark,
and task type is the original CBLUE task type.
Dataset splits Note that after filling in the CBLUE data samples in the prompt templates, the
train/dev/test sets for some task will be quite large. Considering that LLMs require high computation
resources to fine-tune and have large latency for token sequence generation, we limit the training
samples of each task to 3000 to 5000, and the dev/test set to 600-800. We first fill each prompt
template with the samples to construct a large test sample pool and randomly select the promptresponse pairs via uniform sampling.21 In Table 8 of Appendix A.2, we present the dataset statistics
for the PromptCBLUE dataset.
Quality Checking

The quality check of our data are conducted in the following three aspects:

• Ensuring the quality of the prompt templates with the help of the expert panel, as described
above.
• Checking the quality of CBLUE benchmark. During the development of PromptCBLUE
benchmark, we are also helping the CBLUE benchmark to improve the annotations. For
example, we have found that the original QQR task has an in-proper label set, and ask the
CBLUE organizers to re-annotate this task.
• Random sampling. To ensure the data quality, we sampled 5% or 200 of the samples from
each task of PromptCBLUE, and each sample is examined by a group of annotator from
the medical field. Finally, we identify an average of 0.9% mislabeling rate. Based on the
evaluation results in the next section, we believe such an error rate will not significantly
impact the overall evaluation accuracy.

Evaluation Setup

Our evaluations are conducted under the following three settings:

• Few-shot in-context learning setting. Under this setting, we investigate the in-context learning ability Li et al. (2023b); Wang et al. (2023) of LLMs by concatenating demonstrating
instruction-response pairs from the training set to the current instruction. For tasks with
label sets, we concatenate demonstrations containing at least k instances for each label
except the CHIP-CTC task.22 For other tasks, we concatenate the k demonstrations. The
exact value of k varies across tasks due to the different sequence length and label set sizes.
The exact number of k for each task is reported in Table 1.23
• Few-shot fine-tuning learning setting. Note that the selected demonstrations from the previous setting could also be treated as training samples to fine-tune the open-sourced models.
We also conduct LLM fine-tuning with different numbers of samples to investigate how
training sample sizes affect the model test performances.
• Fine-tuning. We will fine-tune a series of open-sourced LLMs on the large scale training set (introduced in Section 3) and report their performances. In terms of the finetuning techniques, we will experiment both full-model fine-tuning and a series of parameter efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods. Note that during fine-tuning, we treat the entire
PromptCBLUE as one task and use only one set of PEFT parameters for predictions of all
the test samples.
Under all the above settings, we ask LLMs to generate output and used a series of regular expressions
to extract the model’s choice.24
Evaluation metrics Since metrics like BLUE or ROUGE Lin (2004) can not properly measure how LLMs perform for some of the PromptCBLUE tasks like medical information extraction
tasks, we use post-processing scripts to transform the output sequences to structured data formats.
PromptCBLUE adopt the following metrics:
• Instance-level strict micro-F1 for medical information extraction tasks, IMCS-V2-SR and
CHIP-MDCFNPC. Here, an instance means a complete piece of information extracted from
the given document. For example, in CMeEE-V2, an instance consists of a entity mention
extracted and its predicted entity label. And in IMCS-V2-SR, an instance consists of two
keys: the entity mention of a symptom, and its status. We adopt the strict metrics, meaning
that the model predicts an instance correctly if and only if it correctly predicts the all the
keys of an instance.
• For medical text classification tasks and the IMCS-V2-DAC tasks, we adopt the macro-F1
score.
• For medical natural language inference tasks, we adopt the micro-F1 score.
• For the medical content generation tasks, we adopt ROUGE-L Lin (2004) as the metric.
Models We assess a collection of well-known language models in different sizes. For commercial
models with publicly available APIs, we evaluate GPT-4 OpenAI (2023), ChatGPT25 , and ChatGLM.26 For open-sourced models, we consider Baichuan-13B as the main model. We also compare this LLM with the following (L)LMs: (a) GPT-2 Chinese27 . (b) Randeng-T5-784M28 . (c)

Fine-tuning methods For fine-tuning the open-sourced LLMs, we do not consider full-model
fine-tuning as in Devlin et al. (2019) due to resource limitation. For parameter efficient fine-tuning,
we adopted the following methods: (a) P-tuning Liu et al. (2021). (b) P-tuning v2 Liu et al. (2022).
(c) Parallel adapter by He et al. (2021). (d) LoRA Hu et al. (2021). (e) AdaLoRA by Zhang et al.
(2023). We adjust the hyper-parameters of these methods so that the tunable parameters accounts
for less than 1% of the LLM backbones’ parameters. The detailed hyper-parameter settings for each
method are provided in Appendix A.3. After a model is fine-tuned, we use beam search (beam size
4) for generating the responses.

prompt, we will ask the LLM to generate the 非上述类型 (not in the given categories) label. Thus

C ONCLUSION

In this paper, we present the PromptCBLUE benchmark, which re-purpose the CBLUE benchmark
to the LLM era. We carefully transfer all the traditional medical natural language processing tasks
into the unified prompt-response generation form. Our PromptCBLUE benchmark provide a good
testbed for Chinese LLMs to test their capabilities in the medical domain and their potentials for
various medical applications. This paper also conduct extensive experiments and case studies to
analyze how the current open-sourced Chinese LLMs perform on PromptCBLUE.

CHIP-CDN This task aims to map the diagnosis terms (query terms) written by doctors to standardized disease terms (target terms) according to a certain medical terminology standard Sun et al.
(2020). The number of standardized terms may exceed 10 thousands. The standard system adopted
in this task is usually the International Statistical Classification of Diseases and Related Health
Problems 10th Revision (ICD-10)34 . ICD-10 has more than 30 thousand disease terms, thus it is
prohibitive to feed all the disease terms into ChatGPT. ICD-10 has different versions in different
countries, and in this work we adopt the ICD-10 Beijing Clinical Trial (version v601)35 . We will
refer to this Chinese version of ICD-10 as ICD-10-Beijing.
Recently, this task is usually addressed by a retrieve-and-rank system Park et al. (2019). Firstly,
a small set of candidate standard terms are retrieved via inverted index (e.g., ElasticSearch) or
a semantic index toolkit like Faiss, then a ranking model will score and rank the relevances of
each query-target term pair. To construct the data samples in CBLUE, for each query term in the
task datasets, we first retrieve candidate target terms using BM25 Robertson & Zaragoza (2009).
The candidate pool consists of the top 10-30 terms retrieved by BM25 not are not in the ground
truth, and the ground truth terms (for 80% of the cases). The candidate pool’s order is shuffled so
that LLMs can not obtain the true answers by just selecting the first few candidates. Thus, under

A.3

H YPER - PARAMETER SETTINGS FOR THE PEFT METHODS

We now elaborate on the hyper-parameter settings for different PEFT methods, and the training
settings.
PEFT Hyper-parameters For P-tuning, we prepend 128 soft prompt tokens to the input. The
learnable prompts are randomly initialized and use the LSTM prompt encoder. For P-tuning-V2,
each layer’s soft prompt is directly randomly initialized without prompt encoder. The prompt length
is also 128 for P-tuning-V2. Adapter is added in parallel to the feed-forward and self-attention
33

Manuscript in progress

module of the LLM, with a bottleneck dimension of 128. LoRA’s rank is set to 24 and is added to
the query, key, value, output weight matrix, and the two weight matrix in the feed-forward module.
AdaLoRA’s initial rank setting is the same with LoRA.
Training settings Each PEFT method is trained with the same training hyper-parameters: warm
up steps are 100, batch size is 64, and the learning rate is 3e-4, the optimizer is AdamW, the learning
schedule is linear decay, gradient clipping is 1, weight decay is 1e-4, and the maximum epoch is 10.
The other settings follow the huggingface Transformers’ default settings. We find that training a few
steps after the dev loss hits the lowest point is helpful for generating better responses. Thus we pick
the checkpoint that is 200 steps after the one with the lowest dev loss.
